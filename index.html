Making synthesis gas hydrogen and carbon monoxide

Process of gasification is a process which converts any type of carbon-based raw material into fuel gas. This process has originated in the nineteenth century in a period which can be characterized by its burgeoning industrial revolution and thirst for sustainable energy solutions. Sir William Grove’s foundational works in the 1830s laid bedrock for introduction of previously mentioned process, which became a later reason for synthetic gas production as Franz Fishcer and Hans Tropsch have refined gasification process techniques and integrated catalytic processes into it. In the beginning of twentieth century facilities dedicated to production of syngas for lighting and head have started opening as its industrial utilization has been figured out. It was a combustible gas so in the start of WW2 its significance arose as it started being used as an alternative for gasoline due to its scarcity. Half million of german cars have been powered on this gas and it was starting to get used more widely in the world which led to investment into technologies and production of it hence leading to advancements in this field. Therefore, the latter part of the twentieth century had a renewed focus on syngas, in the exploration of cleaner and more sustainable energy alternatives. It started being created through different ways like steam reforming of methane, partial oxidation of natural gases, and coal gasification. The ongoing pursuit of cleaner energy sources made its role in the world crucial as it linked conventional and renewable energy sources, helped to reduce carbon emissions, increased accessibility of energy in all areas, and was used to manage wastes.

Steam reforming:

Conditions:

  • Temperature in range of 700 to 1000 degrees celsius
  • 3 to 25 MPa pressure
  • Nickel based catalyst
  • 3:1 to 4:1 steam-methane

That high temperature is the optimal amount to break bonds between methane and steam so for a fast rated reaction it is needed to have this level of temperature. higher pressure increases the density of molecules and accordingly increases the rate of reaction as collisionы will occur more oftenly, nickel significantly increases the rate and efficiency of reaction and steam to methane ratio is important to ensure that there is enough steam for reaction.


In steam reforming of methane, naturally derived methane(CH₄) and water vapor(H₂O) which is purified water, act as the initial reactants. The reaction produces hydrogen (H₂), carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and unreacted methane. Hydrogen is cleaned in processes by using Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA), membrane separation, or cryogenic distillation. While carbon monoxide can be generated and reformed for producing additional hydrogen in the form of the water-gas shift reaction, and carbon dioxide is removed in processes through amine scrubbing or cryogenic separation. Methane is recycled and removed in unreacted form. Water vapor is condensed and cleaned for reuse. All these processes yield efficient extraction and purification of syngas for use in industries.

Coal gasification:

Environment

What if the likely effect on the economy and environment? Making syngas via gasification impacts both the economy and environment, new studies say. Environmentally, producing syngas via biomass and trash can reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 50–70% in relation to burning conventional fuels, as it reduces methane emissions from trash and utilizes CO₂ in processes (Bachmann et al., 2023). For example, bio-waste gasification generates 1.2 kg CO₂ eq./kg of syngas, much less compared to 3.5 kg CO₂ eq./kg for syngas produced via coal (Ayodele et al., 2019). Nevertheless, one of its potential drawdowns is that increased biomass gasification will cause competition for land and additional particulate matter emissions through a 15% chance in case of competition with agricultural foods (Whitney H., 2024).

Economics

Economically, producing syngas assists with having alternative sources of energy. The worldwide syngas market will expand at a 12.45% annual pace between 2024 and 2032, with additional demand for hydrogen and artificial fuels (Whitney H., 2024). In addition, when biomass gasification is paired with electrolysis, it can enhance energy efficiency—reaching 74.1%, in contrast with 64.8% for natural gas—and reduce demand for imported fuels, particularly in regions with abundance in biomass (Muhammad, H. J. et al., 2022). On the contrary, high expenses in erecting steam methane reforming (SMR) factories, with a price tag of up to $600 million, and fluctuations in natural gas price make producing syngas not economically viable in the absence of subsidies (Zahir, B. & Marianne, S. Environmental Consequences

Advantages:

- Reduced GHG Emissions: Syngas produced via biomass and trash reduces greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 50–70% in relation to burning conventional fuels (Bachmann et al., 2023). - Lower Warming Impact Globally: Biomass gasification produces syngas with a global warming potential (GWP) of 1.2 kg CO₂ eq./kg, a much lower value compared to GWP for syngas produced through coal (Ayodele et al., 2019).

Disadvantages:

- Land-Use and Environmental Contamination Challenges: Biomass gasification can cause additional land-use concerns and a 15% increase in particulate matter emissions when competing with agricultural lands (Ayodele et al., 2019).

Economic Impact

Advantages:

- Energy and Expanded Market Diversification: Demand for syngas will expand at a 12.45% annual rate between 2024 and 2032, contributing to diversity in sources of energy (Whitney H., 2024). - Higher Energy Efficiency: Biomass gasification and electrolysis have an efficiency level of 74.1%, and for natural gas, it is 64.8%, conserving imported fuel (Muhammad, H. J. et al., 2022).

Disadvantages:

- High Capital Costs and Volatile Fluctuating Flows of Fluctuating Costs for Natural Gas: It takes significant investments (of up to $600 million) to build SMR factories, and fluctuations in the price of natural gas can make profitable production of syngas challenging in the absence of government incentives (Zahir, B. & Marianne, S. E., 2022). In the future, producing additional syngas will rely on new catalysts and technology in electrolysis. Research indicates that new solid-oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) can produce cleaner syngas and save 20% in terms of energy (Bachmann et al., 2023). Researchers have started testing catalysts such as nickel-ceria-zirconia for enhancing reforming and cutting down costs by 15% (Muhammad, H. J. et al., 2022). With new technology, production can become less costly and utilize more syngas in carbon-neutral fuels. For the environment, utilizing additional bio-waste and mill gas in production can reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 80% in 2030, but studies must target water consumption and toxicity (Ayodele et al., 2019). To promote development, having policies that integrate research with efficient programs for converting waste to energy is critical.
